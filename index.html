"Getting Heavier, Younger"


Why Birth Cohort Differences in Obesity Matter



This series of data visualizations is based on data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES), and work previously published by Joyce Lee, a.k.a. Doctor as Designer and colleagues: Lee JM, Pilli S, Gebremariam A, Keirns CC, Davis MM, Vijan S, Freed GL, Herman WH, Gurney JG. Getting heavier, Younger: Birth cohort effects on obesity over the life course. Int J Obes (Lond) 2010 Apr;34(4):614-23.

There is growing awareness about the problem of overweight and obesity in the US, which is associated wtih increased rates of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and premature mortality. The burden of overweight and obesity is substantial, for both US adults and children.
This graph below shows the burden of overweight and obesity in the US population for children and adults for the year 2012, based on data from NHANES.


Click on the buttons to look at the overall burden of overweight, obesity, and both combined for children and adults. Overweight is defined as an Body Mass Index (BMI) Percentile ≥ 85th% & <95th% for children and a BMI ≥25 & < 30 for adults. Obesity is defined as a BMI ≥95th% for children and a BMI>30 for adults.

Based on these estimates, overweight and obesity in adults seems to be a much bigger problem compared with children. But let’s look at the problem of obesity from a different perspective, and evaluat how obesity is changing across birth cohorts.




“To know where people are today, focus on their generation, not their age”

William Strauss



Let’s look at the obesity data using a different approach. Let’s instead divide people into groups by their year of birth (birth cohorts). and look at rates of obesity for a birth cohort at specific ages over the life course.


For example, this green line shows obesity rates for the 1926–1935 birth cohort over time. When the cohort was 30–39 years of age, they had a 13% burden of obesity. By the time they reached 70–79 years of age, they had a 31% burden of obesity.







This next graph shows the obesity rates for each birth cohort. Click on the buttons for each birth cohort to see their obesity trajectory.

You will see that lines are moving up (higher rates of obesity for a given age) and to the left (a greater duration of obesity over the lifecourse for the younger generation>.
